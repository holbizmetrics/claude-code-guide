  increase), K rose only 9→12. log₂(40)≈5.3, log₂(150)≈7.2 — even O(log n)

  anything dramatic.

  Finding 2 — outlier rate grows with n, and that is surprising.
  - V2 sample: 2/600 = 0.3% at n ∈ {14..60} (and zero at n ≤ 30)
  - Pilot: 5% at n=80, 5% at n=100, 10% at n=150

  Mechanism_coverage = 0 (with c_8 > 0) is becoming common at larger n. At
  n=150, 1 in 10 girth-5 cubic graphs has at least one C_8 that doesn't
  decompose via the paper's four small-cycle mechanisms — even though it does
  decompose with K ≤ 12.

  So the structural picture is: the paper's Theorem 3.4 family (a,b ∈ {5,6,7}) 
  becomes inadequate for an increasing fraction of graphs as n grows, but the 
  failure is bounded — you need cycles up to length ~12, not arbitrarily long.

  Implication for the working hypothesis:
  "a, b ∈ {5..f(n)} with f(n) = O(log n)" — needs revising downward. Looks more
  like "a, b ∈ {5..C} with C ≈ 12" in this n-range. Whether that's a true
  constant or extremely slow growth, we don't know yet.

  Right: text-trained LLMs lack sensorimotor grounding. They have a theory of
  physics, not physical experience. Wrong as binary: there's some model, just
  not the embodied kind. The "4-month-old baby better physics engine than GPT-5"

   for "reasoning about counterfactual physical scenarios in language," GPT-5
  dramatically better. The claim conflates kinds of models.

   at system level.
  You and I are using auto-memory right now — your MEMORY.md index and 27 topic
  files loaded at session start gave me ~30 pieces of prior context. The model
  itself starts blank, but the system doesn't. LeCun's framing is a 2022-era
  observation that 2025-era systems route around with RAG, vector stores, agent
  loops, and structured memory. The goldfish-with-notebook metaphor undersells
  what notebooks actually do.

  #3 No real ability to reason — most contested, partly right, partly 
  defining-the-question.
  Right: LLMs do interpolation over training distribution. They fail in
  characteristic ways under trivial transformations of solved problems (variable
   renames, small symmetry changes). The "textbook problem yes, trivial child
  problem no" claim has real empirical support.
  Overreaching: "Pattern matching against reasoning patterns" can BE reasoning
  if patterns are abstract and deep enough. The strong "no real ability" claim
  is hard to operationalize without circularity. Frontier LLMs solve genuinely
  novel problems on dynamic benchmarks.
  The "skin in the game" point has philosophical weight — values learned from
  text aren't values held in flesh. But it's also a moving target: every time
  LLMs demonstrate situated-seeming behavior, critics narrow the goalposts.

  #4 Limited planning — empirically the most testable, and partly outdated.
  The "meetings on opposite sides of town" failure happens with naive
  autoregressive LLMs without tools. With tools (calendar, maps), structured
  planning (tree-of-thought, agent loops), or proper scaffolding, it's largely
  solved. The deeper point — "not actually simulating the future" — has merit.
  LLMs don't run internal counterfactual simulators the way classical planners
  do. But mechanistic-interp work suggests modern LLMs' hidden-state computation
   does include something search-like.

  The metaphor: "language is a thin compressed shadow of reality. Training on 
  text alone is like learning to swim by reading a textbook."
  This is good rhetoric, partly true. Text isn't just "a shadow" — it's the
  compressed encoding of vast human thought, observation, and theory. Reading a
  textbook teaches a theory of swimming, not swimming. LLMs learn a theory of
  the world. That's real. But the metaphor sells short the structure in
  language: language encodes much more than surface description; it encodes
  reasoning patterns, causal structure, social norms, and theory. A textbook
  doesn't teach you to swim — but a textbook on swimming written by humans
  encodes vast accumulated insight.
